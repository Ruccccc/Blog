Coset

Coset

Definition

Given a subgroup \(H\) of a group \(G\), for \(a \in G\), the set \[ aH = \{ ah : h \in H \} \] is called the left coset of \(H\) containing \(a\).
Similarly, \(Ha\) is called the right coset of \(H\) containing \(a\).

The set of left cosets of \(H\) in \(G\) is denoted by \(G/H\).
The set of rihgt cosets of \(H\) in \(G\) is denoted by \(H\backslash G\)

Properties

For two left cosets \(aH\) and \(bH\), the following hold.

  1. \(aH\) and \(bH\) both have the same cardinality as \(H\).
  2. If \(aH \cap bH\) is non-empty, then \(aH = bH\).
  3. \(aH = bH\) if only if \(a^{-1}b \in H\).
  4. \(G = \bigcup\limits_{gH \in G / H}^{} gH\).

It's similarly for two right cosets.

Example

Let \(G = S_3 = \{e, (12), (13), (23), (123), (132) \}\) and \(H = \{e, (12)\}\)
The left cosets are \(H = \{e, (12)\}\) itself, \((13)H = \{(13), (13)(12)\} = \{(13), (123)\}\) and \((23)H = \{ (23), (23)(12) \} = \{ (23), (132) \}\). The right cosets are \(H = \{e, (12)\}\), \(H(13) = \{(13), (12)(13)\} = \{(13), (132)\}\) and \(H(23) = \{(23), (12)(23)\} = \{(23), (123)\}\)

Indexes of Subgroup

Definition

Let \(H\) be a subgroup of group of a group \(G\). The number of left coset of \(H\) is the index of \(H\) in G deonted by \([G : H]\)

Theorem

Suppose that \(H\) and \(K\) are subgroups of a group \(G\) such that \(K \leq H \leq G\). Suppose that \([G : H]\) and \([H : K]\) are finite. Then \([G : K]\) is alse finite and \([G : K] = [G : H] [H : K]\)

Lagrange's theorem

Theorem (Lagrange’s theorem)

Let \(H\) be a subgroup of a finite group \(G\). Then the order of \(H\) divides the order of \(G\).

Proof

\(G\) is equal to the disjoin union of left cosets of \(H\) and every left coset has the smae cardinality as \(H\). Therefore \[|G| = |H| \times \text{( the number of left cosets) }\] This proves the theorem.

Corollary

The order of an element \(a\) of a finite order group \(G\) divides the order of the group.

Apply Largrange's theorem to \(H = \langle a \rangle\)

Corollary

Every group of prime order is cyclic.

Proof

Let \(g \in G\) be an element not equal to \(e\). Then \(|\langle g \rangle |\) divides the order of \(G\). Since \(|G|\) is a prime, either \(|⟨g⟩| = 1\) or \(|G|\). The former case can not occur because \(g \neq e\). Then \(|⟨g⟩| = |G|\) implies \(⟨g⟩ = G\), i.e., \(G\) is cyclic.

Applications of Lagrange's theorem

Question

Find all the subgroups of \(S_3\)

Solutions

Since \(|S_3| = 6\), by lagrange's theorem, the possible orders of a subgroup \(H\) are \(1,2,3,6\)

  1. Case \(|H| = 1\): \(H = \{e\}\)
  2. Case \(|H| = 2\): Since every group of order 2 is isomorphic to the cyclic group \(\mathbb{Z}_2\), \(H = \langle \sigma \rangle\) for some element \(\sigma\) of order 2. There are three subgroups of order 2. They are \(\{e, (12)\}\), \(\{e, (13)\}\) and \(\{e, (23)\}\).
  3. Case \(|H| = 3\): By the same token, every subgroup of order 3 is cyclic. There are two elements of order 3, namely, \((123)\) ans \((132)\). They both generate \(\{e, (123), (132)\}\).
  4. Case \(|H| = 6\): In this case, \(H = S_3\). Thus, we see that \(S_3\) has \(6\) subgroups.

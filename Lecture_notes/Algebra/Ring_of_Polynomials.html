Ring of Polynomials

Definitions

  1. Let $R$ be a ring. A polynomial $f(x)$ over $R$ is a formal sum
    $$\sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} a_i x^i = a_0 + a_1x + a_2x^2 + \cdots + a_nx^n + \cdots$$
    where $a_i \in R$, and only a finite number $i$ such that $a_i \neq 0$.
  2. The letter $x$ is called an indeterminate.
  3. $a_i$ is the coefficients of $f(x)$.
  4. The lagrest $i$ such that $a_i \neq 0$ is the degree of $f(x)$.

    If all $a_i = 0$, the the degree of $f(x)$ is undefined. (In some books, it’s defined to be $-\infty$).
  5. The zero polynomial and polynomials of degree 0 is called constant polynomials.

Theorem

Let $R$ be a ring.

The set $R[x]$ of all polynomials over $R$ is a ring with the addition and multiplication defined as
$$f(x) + g(x) = \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} (a_i + b_i) x^i$$ $$f(x)g(x) = \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} \left( \sum_{j = 0}^{i} a_{j}b_{i-j} \right) x^i$$
where $f(x) = \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} a_i x^i$ and $g(x) = \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} b_i x^i$ is polynomials over $R$.

Futhermore, if $R$ is communicative, then so is $R[x]$. If $R$ is a ring with unity, the so is $R[x]$ with the multiplicative identity $1_R[x]$ being $1_R$.

Remarks

  1. The ring $R[x_1, \dots , x_n]$ of polynomials in $n$ indeterminates over $R$ can be similarly defined.
  2. If $D$ is an integral domain, then so is $D[x]$.
  3. Let $F$ be a field, the set $F[x]$ is not a field since $x$ is not a unit in $F[x]$.

    On the other hand, the field of fractions of $F[x]$ is
    $$F(x) = { f(x)/g(x) : f(x), g(x) \in F[x], g(x) \neq 0 }$$
    and $F(x)$ is called the field of rational functions in $x$ over $F$.
  4. The field of rational functions in $n$ indetermiates over $F$ is similarly defined.

Remarks

  1. Some properties of polynomials over $\Bbb{R}$ may not hold for polynomials over a general ring $R$.
    1. If $R$ is noncommutative, then $R[x]$ is noncommutative.
    2. For $f(x), g(x) \in \Bbb{R}$, ${\rm deg} f(x)g(x) = {\rm deg} f(x) + {\rm deg} g(x)$.

      But for $R = \Bbb{Z}_4$, $$(\bar 2 x + \bar 1 )^2 = \bar 4 x^2 + \bar 4 x + \bar 1 = \bar 1$$
      so ${\rm deg} (\bar 2 x + \bar 1 )^2 \neq {\rm deg} (\bar 2 x + \bar 1 ) + {\rm deg} (\bar 2 x + \bar 1 )$.
  2. Remark 1.2 above is hold when $R$ is a integral domain.

The First Ring Isomorphism Theorem

Theorem – the first ring isomorphism theorem

Let $\phi : R \to R^\prime$ be a ring homomorphism.

$R / {\rm ker} \phi$ has a canonical ring structure such that $R / {\rm ker} \phi \cong \rho (R^{\prime})$

In general, the kernel of some ring homomorphism is called an ideal.

Theorem – evaluation homomorphism

Suppose $R$ is a subring of $R^\prime$ and $R^\prime$ is commutative. Then for $\alpha \in R^\prime$, we have the ring homomorphism $\phi_\alpha : R[x] \to R^\prime$ by
$$\phi_\alpha (a_0 + a_1x + \dots + a_nx^n) = a_0 + a_1\alpha + \dots + a_n\alpha^n$$
called the evaluation homomorphism.

By the first ring isomorphism theorem, $R[x] / {\rm ker} \phi_\alpha \cong \rho (R[x]) = R[\alpha]$

and $R[\alpha] = {f(\alpha) \mid f(x) \in R[x] }$

Ring Extension

$R[\alpha]$ can be considered as an extension of $R$ by joining an element $\alpha$ from a larger ring $R^\prime$.

The result above show that every ring extension of $R$ by adding single element is isomorphic to $R[x]/I$, where $I$ is a ideal of $R[x]$.

In general, a finitely generated commuatative ring with unity and characteristic $n$ is iosmorphic to $\Bbb{Z}_n[x_1, \dots, x_k] / I$, where $I$ is a ideal of $\Bbb{Z}_n[x_1, \dots, x_k]$

Zero of a polynomial

Definition

Let $F$ be a subfield of a field $E$. Let $f(x) \in F[x]$. Suppose that $\alpha \in E$ is an element such that $f(\alpha) = 0$, then $\alpha$ is a zero of $f(x)$ in $E$.

Example

  1. Let $f(x) = x^2 - 2 \in \Bbb{Q}[x]$, then $\pm\sqrt{2}$ are the zeros of $f(x)$ in $\Bbb{R}$.
  2. Let $f(x) = x^2 + 1 \in \Bbb{R}[x]$, then $\pm i$ are the zeros of $f(x)$ in $\Bbb{C}$.

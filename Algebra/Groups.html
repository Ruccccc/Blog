Group

Definition

A group \((G, \ast)\) is a set G, with a binary operation \(\ast\), such that

  1. \(\ast\) is associative.
  2. There is a indentity element \(e \in G\) for \(\ast\).
  3. \(\forall a \in G\), there is an inverse \(a^\prime\) of \(a\) in \(G\), such that \(a^\prime \ast a = a \ast a^\prime = e\).
  • Denote \((G, \ast)\) by \(G\) for short when there is no ambiguity.

Example

The set \(GL(n, \mathbb{R})\) of all invertible \(n\times n\) matrices under matrix multiplication is a group. (\(GL\) stands for general linear)

Click to expand
  1. Closedness: Recall: An matrix \(A\) is invertible iff \(det(A) \neq 0\) Suppose \(A\) and \(B\) is invertible, then \(det(A), det(B) \neq 0\). \(det(AB) = det(A)det(B) \neq 0\). Therefore, \(\forall A, B \in GL(n, \mathbb{R}) \Rightarrow AB \in GL(n, \mathbb{R})\)
  2. Associative: A porperty of matrix multipliction.
  3. Indentity element: The matrix \(I_n\) satisfies \(AI_n = I_n A = A\)
  4. Inverse: Suppose that \(A \in GL(n, \mathbb{R})\). Then \(A^{-1}\) is also in \(GL(n, \mathbb{R})\) since \(det(A^{-1}) = \frac{1}{det(A)} \neq 0\)

Abelian

Definition

A group \((G, \ast)\) is abelian if its binary operation is commutative.

Properties of Groups

Theorem - Cancellation Law

Let \(G\) be a group. Then the left and right cancellation laws hold in G. That is, \(ab = ac\) implies \(b = c\), and \(ba = ca\) implies \(b = c\) for all \(a, b, c \in G\)

proof

Suppose that \(ab = ac\). Consider the equality \[ (a^{-1}a)b = (a^{-1}a)c \] By the associativeity, we have \[ (a{-1}a)b = (a^{-1}a)c \] Since \(a^{-1}\) is an inverse of \(a\), we have \(a^{-1}a = e\), and thus, \[eb = ec\] follows that \(b = c\). The proof of the assertion that \(ba = ca\) implies \(b = c\) is similar.


Theorem - The equation \(ax = b\)

Let \(G\) be a group. Let \(a\) and \(b\) be elements in \(G\). Then the equations \(ax = b\) and $ya = b have unique solutions \(x\) and \(y\) in \(G\).
However, there are binary structures that \(ax = B\) may not be solvable for all \(a\) and \(b\).

Theorem - Uniqueness of identity and inverse element

Let \(G\) be a group. There is only one element \(e\) in \(G\) such that \(ex = xe = x\) for all \(x \in G\).
Like wise, for each \(a \in G\), there is only one element \(a^{-1}\) in \(G\) such that \(a^{-1} a = a a^{-1} = e\).

Collary

Let \(G\) be a group. For all \(a, b \in G\), we have \((ab)^{-1} = b^{-1} a ^{-1}\).

Subgroup

Defination >If a subset \(H\) of a group \(G\) is closed under the binary operation of \(G\) and if \(H\) with the induced operation from \(G\) is itself a group, then \(H\) is a subgroup of \(G\). >denoted \(H \leq G\) or \(G \geq H\)

Proper subgroup

If \(G\) is a group, then the subgroup consisting of \(G\) itself is the improper subgroup of \(G\). All other subgroups are proper subgroups of \(G\).

The subgroup \(\lbrace e \rbrace\) is the trivial subgroup of \(G\). All other subgroups are nontrivial.

Property of subgroup

  • Indentity and Inverse element

Let \(H\) be a subgroup of \(G\). Then the identity \(e_H\) of \(H\) is equal to the identity \(e_G\) of \(G\). Moreover, for \(a \in H\), the inverse of \(a\) in \(H\) is equal to the inverse of \(a\) in \(G\).

proof

By the definition of \(e_H\) and \(e_G\) , we have \(e_H e_H\) = \(e_H\) = \(e_H e_G\). Applying the cancellation law, we have \(e_H\) = \(e_G\) . For \(a \in H\) , let \(a^\prime\) be the inverse of \(a\) in \(H\), then \[a a^\prime = e_H = e_G = a a^{-1}\] Therefore, \(a^\prime = a^{-1}\).

Criterion of Subgroups

A subset \(H\) of a group \(G\) is a subgroup of \(G\) if and only if

  1. \(H\) is closed under the binary operation of \(G\)
  2. \(H\) contains \(e_G\)
  3. \(\forall a \in H\), the inverse \(a^{-1}\) is also in \(H\)

Cartesian product

Definition

The Cartesian product of sets $S_1, S_2, \ldots, S_n$ is the sets of ordered n-tuples $(a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n)$, where $a_i \in S_i$ for $i = 1,2, \ldots, n$.

It’s denoted by either $$S_1 \times S_2 \times \ldots \times S_n$$ or $$\prod_{i = 1}^{n} {S_i}$$

Direct Products

Theorem

Let $G_1, G_2, \ldots, G_n$ be groups. For $(a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n)$ and $(b_1, b_2, \ldots, b_n)$ in $\prod_{i=1}^{n} G_i$ define $(a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n)(b_1, b_2, \ldots, b_n)$ to be $(a_1b_1, a_2b_2, \ldots, a_{n}b_{n}$ Then $\prod_{i=1}^{n} G_i$ is a group under this binary operation.

Definition

The group $\prod_{i=1}^{n} G_i$ is called the direct product of the groups $G_i$.

Theorem

The group $\mathbb{Z}_m \times \mathbb{Z}n$ is cyclic and isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}{mn}$ if and only if m and n are relatively prime.

**Proof**

$(\implies)$

Suppose the group $\mathbb{Z}_m \times \mathbb{Z}_n$ is cyclic. Let $(\bar a , \bar b )$ be a generator of $\mathbb{Z}_m \times \mathbb{Z}_n$, which is of order $mn$. Let $k = lcm(m, n)$. Then
$$ k(\bar a , \bar b ) = (k\bar a , k\bar b ) = (\bar 0, \bar 0) $$


Therefore $mn \leq k = \text{lcm}(m, n)$ which means $m$ and $n$ are relatively prime.



$(\impliedby)$

Assume that $m$ and $n$ are relatively prime, Then we have $\text{lcm}(m,n) = mn$. let the order of the element $(\bar 1, \bar1)$ to be $k$.
Then $k \leq mn$ and
$$(\bar 0, \bar 0) = k(\bar 1, \bar 1) = (\bar k, \bar k)$$

Therefore, $m|k$ and $n|k$, which means $\text{lcm}(m,n) | k$. Especially, we have $$mn = \text{lcm}(m,n) \leq k \leq mn$$
We conclude that $k = mn$, $(\bar 1, \bar 1)$ is a generator, and $\mathbb{Z}_m \times \mathbb{Z}_n$ is cyclic.

Corollary

The group $\prod_{i = i}^{n} \mathbb{Z}{m_i}$ is cyclic and isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}{m_1 m_2 \ldots m_n}$ if and only if the numbers $m_i$ are pairwise coprime.

Theorem

Let $(a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n) \in \prod_{i=1}^{n} G_i$. If $a_i$ is of finite orer $r_i$ in $G_i$, then the order of $(a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n)$ in $\prod_{i=1}^{n} G_i$ is equal to the least common multiple of all $r_i$.

Examples

  1. What is the order of $(\bar 9, \bar 5, \bar 7)$ in the group $\mathbb{Z}{12} \times \mathbb{Z}{15} \times \mathbb{Z}_{20}$?
Solution

$\bar 9$ in $\mathbb{Z}_12$ is of order $4$.
$\bar 5$ in $\mathbb{Z}_15$ is of order $3$.
$\bar 7$ in $\mathbb{Z}_20$ is of order $20$.
$\text{lcm}(4, 3, 20) = 60$. Thus the order of $(\bar 9, \bar 5, \bar 7)$ is $60$.

  1. For $G_1 = \mathbb{Z}_3 \times \mathbb{Z}_3 \times \mathbb{Z}_9$, it has $9 \times 6 = 54$ elements of order 9.
Solution

There is $6$ elements in $\mathbb{Z}_9$ of order $9$
and all elements of $\mathbb{Z}_3$ is of order $3$.
Therefore, we have $3 \times 3 \times 6$ elements in $G_1$ of order $9$.

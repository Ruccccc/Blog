First Isomorphism Theorem

Theroem

Let $\phi : G \to G^\prime$ be a homomorphism with kernel $K$. Then the canonical map $\mu : G/K \to \phi(G)$ defined by $\mu(gK) = \phi(g)$ is a group isomorphism.

Join of Two Subgroup

Definition

Let $H$ and $N$ be two subgroup of $G$. The join $\langle H, N \rangle$ of $H$ and $N$ is the smallest subgroup of $G$ that contains both $H$ and $N$.

Equivalently, $\langle H, N \rangle$ is the intersection of all subgroups of $G$ containing $H$ and $N$.

Remark

$\begin{align} \langle H, N \rangle &= HN \cup HNHN \cup HNHNHN \cup \cdots \ &= \bigcup_{i = 1}^{\infty} (HN)^i \end{align}$

Theorem

Let $H$ and $N$ be two subgroup of $G$. If $N$ is a normal subgropu of $G$, then $HN = NH = \langle H, N \rangle$ is a subgroup of $G$.

Proof

First we proof that $HN$ is a subgroup of $G$.

  1. Closure: Let $h_1n_1, h_2n_2 \in HN$. Since $N$ is a normal subgroup, $h_2^{-1}n_1h_2 \in N$. Then $h_1n_1h_2n_2 = (h_1h_2)((h_2^{-1}n_1h_2)n_2) \in HN$
  2. Identity: $e = ee \in HN$
  3. Inverse: Let $hn \in HN$. We have $(hn)^{-1} = n^{-1}h^{-1}$. Since $N$ is normal, $hn^{-1}h^{-1} \in N$.
    Thus, $(hn)^{-1} = n^{-1}h^{-1} = h^{-1}(hn^{-1}h^{-1}) \in HN$

Then we proof that $\langle H, N \rangle = HN$

  1. $\langle H, N \rangle \subseteq HN$: trivial
  2. $\langle H, N \rangle \supseteq HN$: Any subset containing $H$ and $N$ must contain all element of the form $hn$, where $h \in H$ and $n \in N$. Therefore we have $\langle H, N \rangle \supseteq HN$

Second Isomorphism Theorem

Theorem

Let $H$ be a subgroup of $G$ and $N$ be a normal subgroup of $G$.
Then $(HN) / N \cong H/(H \cap N)$

**Proof**

Define a function $\phi : H \to (HN)/N$ by $\phi(h) = hN$. We claim that

  1. $\phi$ is a homomorphism
  2. $ker(\phi) = H \cap N$
  3. $\phi$ is onto

By First Isomorphism Theorem, $H/(H \cap N) \cong (HN) / N$

Applications of Second Isomorphism Theorem

Corollary

By Second Isomorphism Theorem, when $|G| < \infty$, we have $|HN| = \dfrac{|N|||H}{|H \cap N|}$

Theorem

Let $G$ be a group with a subgroup $H$ and normal subgroup $N$. If $|H \cap N| = 1$ and $|G| = |H||N|$, then every element of $G$ can be written ws $hn$ for some unique $h \in H$ and $n \in N$.

Remark

If $|H|$ and $|N|$ are coprime, then we always have $|H \cap N| = 1$

Example

Let $G$ be a group of order $2p$, where $p$ is a prime.

By Cauchy’s theorem, there exist an element $a$ of order $2$ and a element $b$ of order $p$. Since $\langle a \rangle$ is of order $2$, it’s a normal subgroup. Therefore, we have
$$\langle a, b \rangle = \langle a \rangle \langle b \rangle = G$$

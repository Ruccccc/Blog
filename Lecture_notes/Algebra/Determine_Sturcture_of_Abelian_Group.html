The fundamental theorem of finitel abelian groups

Theorem

Suppose that $G$ is a finite abelian group. Then $G$ is isomorphic to a direct product of finite cyclic groups in the form
$$\mathbb{Z}{p{1}^{e_1}} \times \mathbb{Z}{p{2}^{e_2}} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}{p{n}^{e_n}}$$
where $p_i$ are primes, not necessary distinct. The direct product is unique except for possible rearrangement of the factors.

$p$-torsion Subgroups

if we collect all cyclic groups in the above express with the same $p_i = p$ to obtain a subgroup $G_p$, called the $p$-torsion subgroup of $G$, which can be also characterized by
$$G_p = { a \in G \mid a^{p^k} = e \text{for some} k \in \mathbb{N}$$
Then we can rewrite the result above as
$$G = \prod_{p \Big| |G|} G_p$$
where $G_p$ is isomorphic to a group of the form
$$\mathbb{Z}{p^{r_1}} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}{p^{r_k}}$$
Therefore, to determine the froup structure of $G$, it is sufficient to determine the froup structure of its $p$-torsion subgroups.

Group Structure of $\mathbb{Z}_m^\times$

We want to study the group structure of $\mathbb{Z}m^\times$. First, we need to find the cardinality of the group.
Recall that when $m_1, m_2$ are coprime. The map
$$rho : \mathbb{Z}{m_1 m_2} \mapsto \mathbb{Z}{m_1} \times \mathbb{Z}{m_2}$$
is an additive group isomorphism. Note that this map also preserves another binary structure, multiplication.
Recall that
$$\begin{align}
\mathbb{Z}m^\times &:= { \bar a \in \mathbb{Z}m \mid \bar a \text{a has the multiplicative inverse} } \
&= { \bar a \in \mathbb{Z}m, {\rm gcd}(a, m) = 1 }
\end{align}$$
Thus, we have $\mathbb{Z}{m_1 m_2}^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}{m_1}^\times \times \mathbb{Z}{m_2}^\times$

Cardinality of $\mathbb{Z}_m^\times$

Theorem

The cardinality of $\mathbb{Z}m^\times$ is equal to $\m \prod{p} (1 - 1/p), where $p$ runs through all prime divisors of $m$.

Proof

If $m = p^k$, a prime power, we have
$$\begin{align}
|\mathbb{Z}{p^k}^\times| &= #{\bar a \in \mathbb{Z}{p^k} \mid {\rm gcd}(a, p^k) = 1 }
&= #{ 0 \leq i < p^k \mid {\rm gcd}(i, p^k) = 1 }
&= #{ 0 \leq i < p^k \mid {\rm gcd}(i, p) = 1 }
&= p^{k-1}(p-1)
\end{align}$$
If $m = p_1^{k_1} \cdots p_r^{k_r}$ where $p_i$ are distinct primes and $k_i > 0$, then
$$|\mathbb{Z}m^\times| = \prod{i = 1}^{r} p_i^{k_i}(1 - {1 \over p_i}) = m \prod_{i = 1}^{r} (1 - {1 \over p_i})$$

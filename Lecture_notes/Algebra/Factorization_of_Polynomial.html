Division Algorithm

Theorem

Let $\Bbb{F}$ be a field and $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ be two polynomial in $\Bbb{F}[x]$.

Suppose that $g(x)$ is not zero polynomial. Then exist unique polynomial $q(x)$ and $r(x)$ such that
$$f(x) = g(x)q(x) + r(x)$$
and ${\rm deg}; r(x) < {\rm deg}; g(x)$.

**Proof**

Notation

If a polynomial $f(x)$ can be express as multiple of two polynomial $g(x)h(x)$, then we say that $g(x)$ and $h(x)$ is the factor of $f(x)$.

We then denote that $g(x) \mid f(x)$.

Example

Let $\Bbb{F} = \Bbb{Z}_5$, $f(x) = x^4 + x^2 + 3x + 2$ and $g(x) = x^2 + 2x + 3$.

– 待補

Remark

Note that the condition that $\Bbb{F}$ is a field is crucial.

For example, consider $\Bbb{Z}[x]$ instead. Let $f(x) = x + 1$ and $g(x) = 2x$. Then there is no $q(x), r(x) \in \Bbb{Z}[x]$ such that $f(x) = g(x)q(x) + r(x)$ with ${\rm deg}; r(x) < {\rm deg}; g(x)$

However, the algorithm still work for integral domains if the leading coefficient of $g(x)$ is invertible.

Zero of Polynomials

Theorem

Let $\Bbb{F}$ be a field. An element $a \in \Bbb{F}$ is a zero of $f(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$ if and only if $x - a$ is a factor of $f(x)$ in $\Bbb{F}[x]$.

**Proof**

($\implies$)

Suppose that $f(a) = 0$. By division algroithm, $f(x) = (x - a)q(x) + r(x)$ for some polynomial $q(x), r(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$ with ${\rm deg}; r(x) < {\rm deg};(x - a) = 1$. Therefore, we can write $r(x) = c$ as a constant polynomial.
$$f(a) = 0 = 0q(x) + r(x) = c$$
Then we can say that $x - a$ is a factor of $f(x)$.

($\impliedby$)

Suppose that $x - a$ is a factor of $f(x)$. We have $f(x) = (x - a)q(x)$ for some polynomial $q(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$

Then $f(a) = 0q(x) = 0$, which says that $a$ is a zero of $f(x)$.


Corollary

Let $\Bbb{F}$ be a field. A nonzero polynomial $f(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$ of degree $n$ can have at most $n$ distinct zeros in $\Bbb{F}$.

**Proof**

Proof by induction

When $f(x)$ has degree $0$, $f(x) = c$ for some $c \neq 0$ and it’s obvious that $f(x)$ has no zero.

Suppose that the statement holds for all polynomial with degree less then $k$, we now prove that the statement holds for polynomials with degree $k + 1$.

If $f(x)$ has no zeros, we are done. Otherwise, assume that $a \in \Bbb{F}$ is a zero of $f(x)$.

By the previous Theorem, $f(x) = (x - a)q(x)$ where $q(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$ with degree $k$.

Suppose that $b \in \Bbb{F}$ is another zero of $f(x)$, then $f(b) = (b - a)q(b) = 0$.

Since $b \neq a$ and $\Bbb{F}$ has no zero diviser, $q(b) = 0$. And this implies that $a$ is the only zero of $f(x)$ which is not zero of $q(x)$.

By the induction hypothesis, the polynomial $q(x)$ has at most $k$ distinct zeros in $\Bbb{F}$. Therefore, $f(x)$ has at most $k+1$ distinct zeros in $\Bbb{F}$.


Corollary

Let $\Bbb{F}$ be a field. If $G$ is a finite subgroup of the multiplicative group $\Bbb{F}^\times$. Then $G$ is cyclic. In particular, if $\Bbb{F}$ is a finite field, then $\Bbb{F}$ is cyclic.

Example

  1. In $\Bbb{Z}_5$, we have $2^2 = 4$, $2^3 = 3$ and $2^4 = 1$. Thus, $\Bbb{Z}_5^\times = \langle 2 \rangle$ is cyclic.
  2. We have $|\Bbb{Z}_7^\times| = 6$. Thus, the multiplicative order of an element in $\Bbb{Z}_7^\times$ is $1$, $2$, $3$ or $6$. Now $3^2 = 2 \neq 1$ and $3^3 = 6 \neq 1$. Therefore, the multiplicaive order of $3$ is $6$, and $\Bbb{Z}_7^\times = \langle 3 \rangle$ is cyclic.
**Proof**

Irreducible Polynomial

Definition

Let $\Bbb{F}[x]$ be a field.

If a nonconstant polynomial $f(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$ can’t be expressed as a product $g(x)h(x)$ of two polynomial in $\Bbb{F}[x]$ with $0 < {\rm deg}; g(x), {\rm deg}; h(x) < {\rm deg}; f(x)$, then $f(x)$ is irrducible over $\Bbb{F}$ or is an irreducible polynomial in $\Bbb{F}[x]$.

If a polynomial $f(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$ is not nonconstant and not irreducible, then it’s reducible over $\Bbb{F}$

Remark

  1. If $f(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$ is irreducible, then in $f(x) = g(x)h(x)$, one of $g(x)$ and $h(x)$ has degree equal to $f(x)$ and the other one has degree 0.
    Since the nonzero constant polynomial in $\Bbb{F}[x]$ are precisely units $\Bbb{F}[x]$, the definition of irreducible polynomials can also be given as “f(x) is irreducible over $\Bbb{F}[x]$ if in any factorzation $f(x) = g(x)h(x)$ in $\Bbb{F}[x]$, one of $g(x)$ or $h(x)$ is a unit”.
  2. Note that whether a polynomial is irreducible or not depends on the field we discuss. For example, $x^2 + 1$ is irreducible in $\Bbb{R}$ but reducible in $\Bbb{C}$.

Theorem – Polynomial in degree 2 or 3

Suppose that $f(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$ is of degree 2 or 3. Then $f(x)$ is reducible over $\Bbb{F}$ if and only if $f(x)$ has a zero in $\Bbb{F}$.

**Proof**

($\implies$)

For a polynomial $f(x)$ of degree $2$ or $3$, suppose it is reducible, then $f(x) = g(x)h(x)$ with $0 < {\rm deg}; g(x), {\rm deg}; h(x) < {\rm deg}; f(x) = 2 \text{or} 3$.

Therefore, one of $g(x)$ and $h(x)$ must be of degree 1 taking the form $x - a$ for some $a \in \Bbb{F}$.

Thus, $f(x)$ has a zero $a$ in $\Bbb{F}$.

($\impliedby$)

If $f(x)$ has a zero $a$, then $f(x) = (x - a)g(x)$, which implies that $f(x)$ is reducible.


Example

Determine whether $f(x) = x^3 + x + 1$ is irreducible over $\Bbb{Z}_5$.

Since $f(x)$ is of degree 3, by previous theorem, it’s irreducible if and only if it has a zero in $\Bbb{Z}_5$.

$$\begin{align}
f(0) &= 1 \
f(1) &= 3 \
f(2) &= \overline{8 + 2 + 1} &= \bar 1 \
f(3) &= \overline{27 + 3 + 1} &= \bar 4 \
f(4) &= \overline{64 + 4 + 1} &= \bar 4 \
\end{align}$$
And non of them equal to 0.
Therefore, $f(x)$ is irreducible over $\Bbb{Z}_5$.

Theorem – Gauss’s Lemma (irreduciblility)

Let $f(x) \in \Bbb{Z}[x]$. Then $f(x)$ can factor into a product of two polunomials of lower degrees $r$ and $s$ in $\Bbb{Q}[x]$ if and only if $f(x)$ can factor into a product of two polynomials of lower degrees $r$ and $s$ in $\Bbb{Z}[x]$

**Proof**

Corollary

Let $f(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + \ldots + a_n x^n \in \Bbb{Z}[x]$ with $a_0 \neq 0$. If $f(x)$ has a zero in $\Bbb{Q}$, then the zero is equal to $\dfrac{a}{b}$ where $a \mid a_0$ and $b \mid a_n$.

Polynomial Modulo

Lemma – reduction modulo $m$

Let $m > 1$ be a positive inetger. Define $\phi_m : \Bbb{Z}[x] \to \Bbb{Z}_m[x]$ by
$$\phi_m(a_n x^n + \ldots + a_0) = \bar a_n x^n + \ldots + \bar a_0$$
Then $\phi_m$ is a ring homomorphism, called reduction modulo $m$

When there is no ambiguity, we denote $\bar a_i$ for short.

Lemma

A polynomial $f(x)$ is irreducible over $\Bbb{Q}$ if $f(x)$ is irreducible over $\Bbb{Z}_p$ for some prime $p$.

**Proof**

Suppose that $f(x) = g(x)h(x)$ is reducible over $\Bbb{Q}$, then $\bar f(x) = \bar g(x) \bar h(x)$. Thus, if $\bar f(x)$ is irreducible over $\Bbb{Z}_p$, then $f(x)$ is irreducible over $\Bbb{Q}$


Eisenstien Criterion

Theorem – Eisenstien Criterion

For a polynomial $f(x) = a_n x^n + \ldots + a_0 \in \Bbb{Z}[x]$. If there exist a prime $p$ such that

  • $a_n \not\equiv 0 \pmod p$
  • $a_i \equiv 0 \pmod p$ for all $0 \leq i < n$
  • $a_0 \not\equiv 0 \pmod p$

then $f(x)$ is irreducible over $\Bbb{Q}$.

**Proof**

Cyclotomic Polynomial

Definition

Let $n$ be a integer. The $n$-th cyclotomic polynomial is defined to be
$$\Phi_n(x) = \prod_{\substack{k = 1 ~ n \ {\rm gcd}(k,n) = 1}} (x - \zeta_n^k) = \prod_{\substack{a \in \Bbb{C}^\times \ {\rm ord}(a)=n}} (x - a)$$
where $\zeta_n = {\rm exp}(\dfrac{2i\pi}{n})$.

For example:

$\Phi_1(x) = x - \zeta_1 = x - 1$

$\Phi_2(x) = x - \zeta_2 = x + 1$

$\Phi_3(x) = (x - \zeta_3)(x - \zeta_3^2) = x^2 + x + 1$

$\Phi_4(x) = (x - \zeta_4)(x - \zeta_4^3) = x^2 + 1$

$\Phi_5(x) = (x - \zeta_5)(x - \zeta_5^2)(x - \zeta_5^3)(x - \zeta_5^4) = x^4 + x^3 + x^2 + x + 1$

$\Phi_6(x) = (x - \zeta_6)(x - \zeta_6^5) = x^2 - x - 1$

Remark

Observe that
$$x^n - 1 = \prod_{\substack{a \in \Bbb{C}^\times \ a^n = 1}} (x - a) = \prod_{d \mid n} \prod_{\substack{a \in \Bbb{C}^\times \ {\rm ord}(a) = d}} (x - a) = \prod_{d \mid n} \Phi_d(x)$$
Therefore, we can compute $\Phi_n$ inductively.

For example
$$\begin{align}
\Phi_{10}(x) &= \dfrac{x^{10} - 1}{\Phi_1(x)\Phi_2(x)\Phi_5(x)} \
&= \dfrac{x^{10} - 1}{(x - 1)(x + 1)(x^4 + x^3 + x^2 + x + 1)} \
&= x^4 - x^3 + x^2 - x + 1 \
\end{align}$$

Corollary

Let $p$ be a prime. Then the $p$-th cyclotomic polynomial is
$$\Phi_p(x) = x^{p-1} + x^{p-2} + \ldots + 1 = \dfrac{x^p - 1}{x - 1}$$

Remark

The zeros of $\Phi_p(x)$ are precisely the $p$-th roots of unity, except for 1.

Corollary

The $p$-th cyclotomic polynomial is irrducible over $\Bbb{Q}$

**Proof**

Theorem

The polynomial $x^{p^n} - x$ is equal to the product of all monic irreducible polynomial over $\Bbb{Z}_p$ with degree $d$ dividing $n$.

Common Divisors of Polynomials

Notation

Let $f(x)$ be a polynomial.

Let the set of divisors of $f(x)$ to be $$D(f) = {h(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x] : h(x) \mid f(x) }$$

Remark

If $f(x)$ is irreducible, then $D(f) = \Bbb{F}^\times \cup \Bbb{F}^\times f(x)$

Definition

Let $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ be two polynomial. Let the common divisors of $f$ and $g$ to be
$$CD(f,g) = D(f) \cap D(g)$$

A polynomial $h_0(x) \in CD(f,g)$ is called the greatest common divisor of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ if
$${\rm deg}; h_0(x) \geq {\rm deg}; h(x) \forall h(x) \in CD(f,g)$$

Theorem

Let $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ be two polymomials in $\Bbb{F}[x]$ with $f(x)g(x) \neq 0$. Then there exist a unique gcd of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ upto units.

Two nonzero polynomials is called coprime if $1$ is the gcd of them.

Theorem

For a gcd $h(x)$ of polynomials $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ in $\Bbb{F}[x]$ with $f(x)g(x) \neq 0$, there exist $a(x), b(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$ such that
$$a(x)f(x) + b(x)g(x) = h(x)$$

Since there exist division algorithm on $\Bbb{F}[x]$, we can use Euclidean Algorithm to find the gcd of two polynomials just like find the gcd of two integers.

Example

Let $f(x) = 2x^4 + 3x^3 + 2x^2 + 5x + 3$ and $g(x) = 2x^3 + 7x^2 + 8x + 3$ in $\Bbb{Q}[x]$.

Find the gcd of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$.

$$\begin{align}
f(x) &= g(x)(x - 2) + 8x^2 + 18x + 9 \
g(x) &= (8x^2 + 18x + 9)({x \over 4} + {5 \over 16}) + {x \over 8} + {3 \over 16} \
(8x^2 + 18x + 9) &= ({x \over 8} + {3 \over 16})(64x + 48) + 0 \
\end{align}$$
Therefore $\dfrac{x}{8} + \dfrac{3}{16}$ is ${\rm gcd}(f, g)$ and so are $2x + 3$ and $x + \dfrac{3}{2}$

Theorem

Let $p(x)$ be an irreducible polynomial in $\Bbb{F}[x]$. If $p(x) \mid r(x)s(x)$ for $r(x), s(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$, then $p(x) \mid r(x)$ or $p(x) \mid s(x)$ in $\Bbb{F}[x]$.

**Proof**

Suppose $p(x) \not\mid r(x)$. Since $p(x)$ is irreducible, $1$ is the gcd of $p(x)$ and $r(x)$.
Then we have
$$1 = a(x)p(x) + b(x)r(x)$$
$$s(x) = a(x)p(x)s(x) + b(x)r(x)s(x)$$
for some polynomial $a(x), b(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$.
Thus, $s(x)$ is dividable by $p(x)$.


Unique factorization in $\Bbb{F}[x]$

Theorem

Every nonconstant polynomial $f(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$ can be factored into a product of irreducible polynomials in $\Bbb{F}[x]$.
The factorzation is unique upto order and units.

Remark

Upto order means that $f(x)g(x)$ and $g(x)f(x)$ are consider the same.

Upto unit means that $f(x)g(x)$ and $(c(f))(c^{-1}g(x))$ are consider the same, where $c \neq 0 \in \Bbb{F}[x]$.

Ring of Residue Classes

Definition

Let $\Bbb{F}$ be a field and $f(x), g(x), h(x) \in \Bbb{F}[x]$.

We say $g(x)$ and $h(x)$ is in same residue class modulo $f(x)$, denoted by
$$g(x) \equiv h(x) \pmod{f(x)}$$
and we denote the residue class of $g(x)$ by $\overline{g(x)} = g(x) + f(x)\Bbb{F}[x]$.

The residue classes modulo $f(x)$ forms a ring, with operator $+$ and $\cdot$ defined by
$$\overline{g(x)} + \overline{h(x)} := \overline{g(x) + h(x)}$$
$$\overline{g(x)} \cdot \overline{h(x)} := \overline{g(x) \cdot h(x)}$$


The ring is denoted by $\Bbb{F}[x]/f\Bbb{F}[x]$.

Note that $\Bbb{F}[x]/f\Bbb{F}[x]$ is always a commutative ring with unity.

Theorem

A polynomial $f(x)$ is irreducible over $\Bbb{F}[x]$ if and only if $\Bbb{F}[x]/f\Bbb{F}[x]$ is a field.

Example

In $\Bbb{Z}_2[x]$, let $f(x) = x^2 + x + 1$
Since $f(0) = f(1) = 1$, $f(x)$ is irreducible.
Therefore $\Bbb{Z}_2[x] / f\Bbb{Z}_2 = { \bar 0, \bar 1. \bar x, \overline{x+1} }$ is a field with $4$ elements.

Lemma

Let $f(x) \in \Bbb{Z}_p$ where $p$ is a prime and ${\rm deg}; f(x) = n$.
$$\left| \Bbb{Z}_p[x] / f\Bbb{Z}_p \right| = \text{ # the polynomials with}; {\rm deg} < n ; \text{in}; \Bbb{Z}_p = p^n$$
